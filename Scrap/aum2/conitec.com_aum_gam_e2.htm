Current Acknex Games:

            THE NAME OF THE BROTHER


 

Tips and hints regarding ACKNEX games are always welcome, as we're certainly not able to keep an eye on the entire world market.
 


 

The second game getting reviewed here is called THE NAME OF THE BROTHER and was produced by a group of students from Vienna. Properly seen it is actually the first real 3D adventure produced with the ACKENX engine, as QUESTE has still not reached completion. So mouse cursor and inventory are new here - as well as the dialogue management.

The story
 
harks back to 1984 and transports the player to a totalitarian country. Everywhere there are posters admonishing the citizens to behave in a certain way (much like the old-time GDR cheerers like "plan fulfilled!"), and the producers have succeeded in creating a threatening and harsh atmosphere of control and supervision. Now and then death squads touch down with their helicopters in the park and arrest somebody or other. You're quite happy not to be among the victims - for now. Because the player is unfortunately asked to contact a subversive group and somehow acquire paper for printing an illegal flyer. Which leads you right into the thick of it.....

Textures
 
were chosen with care and always scaled and calculated correctly. As THE NAME OF THE BROTHERdoes for a change not take place on a space stations of somewhere fantasy, but in a realistic urban environment, the graphics had to be right on the mark - and they are. With how much utter care and attention the tiles were placed can be seen on the screenshot below, which was taken from a common corridor of a high rise. But then again we are probably met with too few different textures, which renders the game a bit dull visually. Most textures could also have done with quite a bit of smoothing, as they sometimes are bit too pixely, like for example the image from the old part of town here!
 
ACTORS

The actors are a bit two-dimensional and painted the way one finds them in common picture books. That makes them appear rather wooden and immobile. Models would have been the superior choice here, but that feature of the engine was probably introduced too late for the producers. The only models used are the quite splendid signs for the Underground and telecommunications. The communications entities inside the so called "Freenet" (some sort of communications-cyberspace) are of course also models, but are not perceived as such. The number of actors could also easily have been increased by factor four or five. As it is, the locations often appear to be quite deserted. As the levels are usually quite small, the number of actors would not have had weighed down on the performance.
 

Nice Locations

Those living in a big city expect to find some form of underground transportations in games like this. Voilá: here it is. In regular intervals a probably shifted region shaped like an underground comes along and waits for theplayer to board the train. If he hesitates too long, he is still not left out in the cold: the intervals between trains are a lot shorter than in real life! Using the underground as the centre of the game is quite clever indeed, as it stabilizes the performance: the train is the only way for the player to get from one place to the other - while the level is quickly being loaded.
 
User Prompting

User prompting leaves nothing to be desired. Right from the beginning the player is interactively introduced to all functionsof the game, effectively making a handbook superfluous. Even somebody with little experience with 3D games feels at home quite fast. Immediately at the beginning the raven is introduced as a universal support tool and explains to the player how to use the rucksack: simply click on the desired object and off it goes into the rucksack.
 

Sound

isn't all that convincing either. The main title theme (luckily) only appears during the first starting and sounds like a kraut version of Nirvana or some other contemporary independent groove. It probably suits the subversive character of the game, but is certainly not everybody's cup of tea. All I can say is: whoever screams like that shouldn't complain when he is arrested! At least the soundtrack suits the overall theme. The rest of the game presents us with a CD soundtrack that is sometimes quite moody, but sometimes quite annoying as well. The sound on the CD is mixed way too loud to begin with. Sadly the volume may with this version of the engine only be changed with the Win95 multimedia controlboard.
And there's bad grades for the SFX. If you can hear anything at all it's with a lot of hiss, the quality not being top notch at all. Obviously good sound does not come cheap. You just can't do it professionally with a run-of-the-mill 16-bit card, equipped with CoolEdit, a small microphone and a few 8 bit samples from the internet. You just need to do some engineering on stuff like that in a proper studio - something which you just can't expect from a game like NoB. Still - the game could have used a little bit more sound - the unnatural silence sometimes makes it appear a bit sterile. Vocal output of the dialogues wouldn't have been such a bad idea either. The game would have benefited from them, and they wouldn't have been too complicated to do. As it is you only hear them from the announcer on the tube, sounding rather tight.  But the counter-declaration of the developers, wich reached us at 11th of january 98 sounds like this: "Direct speech wich our account of dialogues would have meant to use too much memory and a bigger effort of administration. So we would have been lucky to be able to play all WAVE-Files directly from the CD, wich is unfortunally impossible today. Furthermore the player had to be able to speek too, wich would mean to our mongrel stock player to cast double abundance at all. All in all not so easy." Okay, okay - we apologize and ask the jury to ignore our last remark.

AND SO?

The appeal of The Name of the Brother rest on it's extraordinary concept and it's originality and wittiness. The game is thoroughly styled from beginning to end and thus assumes quite some credibility. The special touch results from the depiction of our real world, expectations are almost completely met. 
Considering how much work and devotion went into this project over several years, a project which from the outset was not meant to result in a commercial product but was exclusively intended to transport a political idea I can only lift my hat and pay respect. Quite a feat! Even though there are a few things to criticize (cf. sounds and actors) one needs to point out that the producers succeeded in setting themselves apart from the host of the usual 3D Shoot-em-Ups as far as the idea and the ambient are concerned. And considering that they are actually giving it away at a price just covering theirs costs there is actually no reason why you shouldn't go out and get the game. Unless you are a big fan of oppressive governments or a dictator yourself, that is.
