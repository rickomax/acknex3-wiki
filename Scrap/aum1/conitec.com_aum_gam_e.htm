Actual Acknex Games:

            ANGST



Welcome to the games column. Here we would like to introduce games that were produced using the Acknex toolkit. What you won't find here is a critique in the style of a game magazine, evaluating the games according to different criteria like "music", "graphics" etc. and a final score for "fun'n'excitement;". We will hand out percentages, though, but no final score, and the criteria will be concerning the necessities of the ACKNEX game engine. So even if the graphics should be beyond redemption, we won't critizise the quality of the graphics, but whether the texture was used in a sensible and correct way.

As we won't be able to review the entire world market, we are always grateful for suggestions and having our attention drawn to ACKNEX games.



The first game we are going to talk about in this column is called ANGST (wich means Fear) and was produced by ManMachine Games from Munich (see also our interview with MGI AndreasVahsen in this issue).
To say it straight away: We have found ANGST to be provokingly bad. Most entries to our level designer competition 1996 had a distinctly higher standard of quality than this game.
All the more surprising, that the game apparently sells quite well, even though the best score it got in a game magazine was 17%. How does that kind of lousy score come about? For once certainly because only 1 level designer was responsible for the whole show. It is simply impossible to push a truly professional game into the market in only five months if you're on your own. The question arises whether quality is indeed necessary, as long as the marketing works. Which is certainly the case with MGI .

Peter Ustinov was once asked why he wouldn't write film critques. He answered "because I know how much work it takes even to produce a bad film." And Alan Parker wrote in his production diary for 'The Commitments': "Whether the movie is great or lousy, I have to get a damn early in the morning!" That is why we won't simply and gleefully take the game apart, but try to pin down the problems.

Textures don't fit

With textures one should always make sure that the player knows what he sees. That he won't have to guess too hard, relying on the context only. A wall arranged like glass bricks, the separate parts merely distinguishing themselves by being black without being the slightst bit transparent, does only generate confusion. Renaissance facades next to metal contruction looking like a dreadnaught simply appear out of context.
The problem ist, that the player does not get something that looks like it could be real. Virtuality of an apparently real is the essence of 3D Games. Even when visualizing a totaly futuristic and out-off-this-world scenario from another planet, an Auntie-Gertie flower pattern right next to a star cruiser control panel is going to unhinge us. Even a thoroughly boring car-park fitted with a single concrete structure has more mood and appears more convincing than that. In ANGST there are hardly two textures that go well together.


Textures were not aligned

WED does include a simple feature: Alignment. Walls are selected ceonsequtively (holding down SHIFT). All you have to keep in mind is that all side's noses (Right-Sides) are all pointing in the same direction, i.e. the player's point of view. If they are not, you can flip them with "F". After that all that remains to be done is pressing that one humble button "A" and all walls are aligned. Why this simple and grateful feature was in this case used with so much reluctance remains a mystery.

Animated actors all wrong

As for the first aggressor - some kind of ghost sceleton - the first thing to strike you is that he has a few faulty pixels (the palette is wrong in this case, same is true for the tank - again proving the point abot the same old adaptation problem!). Apart from that does the sceleton (as is the way of Sprites) always present the player one and the same side. With round and immobile objects this does not make much of a difference, but an animated actor with a WAY should by all means be at least defined with 8 SIDES. Else he will appear to move on invisible tracks. The delay of the texture phases (i.e. of the duration of the phases) should should by all means be adapted to the actor SPEED. The sceleton appears like a roller skater without the skates.

Long objects with only one side

I have already pointed out the Sprites always present the viewer with the same side. Thus, in games like Doom, Duke Nukem, or Skapahnder you won't find any long objects like park benches, office desks, or sideboards. All these longish objects will give the impression that they are turning around, following the player movements. In the first level of ANGST only the well near the beginning fulfils this necessity. Still, the water in the well was sadly left un-animated.

Sky with wrong Offset

As a sky texture is never scaled nor tiled, it's size needs to be adapted to the relative position. This is done by assigning a Y-OFFSET. If you ignore that or misadjust the offset (too high or too low), the sky may appear downwardly distorted to the player once he exceeds a certain height above the region's edge. ANGST produces ungainly stripes during the elevator ride in the first level. If the size of the image does not suffice to fill the background, so that no offset can be gauged, one should at least give a single colour to the lowest line of pixels. It should preferably be black in order to avoid producing stripes. NOTE: the uppermost and the lowest pixel of a SKY is prolonged endlessly by the Engine.

Texture- and Region Heights

ANGST quite frequently confronts us with stone or metal tiles. In almost all cases this textures appear cut off at the upper or lower edges. I admit that measuring out a textures does take some mathematical aptitude. About as much as calculating how to make your wallpaper fit the wall .

If a wall was assigned the regional height of 12 steps and the texture' s original has a height of 128, you will get a scaling factor of 10,6 (128/12 = 10,66). As our stupid engine calculates the wall offsets from absolute zero only, the wall would only fit if our region had the floor height of 0. If that is not the case, the offset of the wall will have to be shifted until the texture fits exactly. In order to do that you'd best create an "Adjust-wdl" that is simply included at the beginning of the WDL. The shoot instruction then easily enables you to shift the x- and y- offset of a wall and have the values output on a panel. We will construct such an adjust panel in the next issue. 

Mood and Ambient

Even the very first ACKNEX demo of oP group used shadow and light. The Michelangelo level for the book GameStudio 3D even had areas of coloured light on the floor. All of this was achieved with AMBIENT values high on contrast either assigned to the floor-texture or the region itself with the drawback that everything - floor, ceiling and walls - would be given that ambient. This rather simple technique allows you to model moody surroundings. ANGST almost entirely foregoes using AMBIENTs, even regions below bridges, that may indeed be expected to be somewhat darker, sport the same brightness as the surrounding region. BTW: the winner of the latest contest did quite a job working with regions of shadow and light.

RESULT

The game story does neither hook nor does it bug anybody who likes to play 'shoot-em-ups' like this one.
The graphics of course are partly flawed. The sound quality is within the range of professionality and anyway, more than 11khz and 8 Bit are not recommended when using Acknex, as the memory would otherwise overflow. The rendered opening titels are quite presentable indeed and the packaging is even first class. As matter of fact, the super title "ANGST" appears to be the single decisive selling proposition. This game is a good example for a poor product selling well through good sales and a top-notchhervorragendes packaging.
Still, ANGST is a botched job produced quick and dirty with an eye on getting it on the market fast. Neither was the concept developed sufficiently, nor did anybody invest too much time in ACTOR behaviour. Good ideas (you may set the tanks on opponents) are rather hard to grasp and distributed more or less at random. For example there is an actor in the first level shooting down the player while persistently missing him. The story does not really come across, and one asks oneself involnutarily what this is all about. Even smart ideas like the propeller plane in the final level go down unheede in the general pixel hodge-podge, especially as it can apparently not be shot down. Afterwards i spent considerable time trying to dive without any results, looking for the entrance to a huge fortress and getting killed each time I entered acertain region without ever finding out why.
The abilites of the engine were hardly realized: performance is below par despite low memory use, and the function of the several game elements like buttons and lifts is left entirely in the dark. Sometimes something happens on the space bar, sometimes nothing does, sometimes the engine returns an indisputable WDL error. Betatesting obviusly failed in this case, if it happened in the first place. For Conitec this game is not much of a reference.

In the next issue we will therefore turn to a more positive example the anti-fascist sponsor game "Der Name der Bruders", which does not propose to professionalism as much, but indeed delivers it rather more.
