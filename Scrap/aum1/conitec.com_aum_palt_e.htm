Defining palettes with Adobe Photoshop 4.0
under Windows 95

by Markus Fabian


    Defining a palette confronts newcomers to the area of 3D production (like for example the author of this little introduction) with apparentyl unsolveable problems: how do I get a grip on the colour shading that is elemental for shading?
    The following will introduce you step-by-step to a way of using the Adobe Photoshop to produce your own palette as PCX file and how to colour your images with it. As the extemporations are a little detailed, you might want make a hard copy of the page and go through it offline.

Step 1

    Start the Adobe Photoshop and chose FILE/NEW.... In the following menue chose 160 x 160 pixel as the size of the image and RGB Color as its colour. As we are going to need an image with 256 colours as a palette, chose IMAGE/MODE/INDEXED COLOR.... In the following menue chose CUSTOM as palette, as we want to design our own palette. it will appear right away, showing only black quadrangles, which we will intially accept right away with OK.

Step 2

    Before starting to manipulate the single colours you should do two things:

    a) chose a Colour Picker
    b) decide on the distribution of the colours on the palette.

    The Color Picker is the very menue you will use to mix your colours. Adobe Photoshop uses its own picker as default, it allows you to define your colours in all possible ways. If you're not an illustrator and do not know the various terms, or simply want to follow this example, use the Color Picker of MS Paint - you can select it under FILE/PREFERENCES/GENERAL....

    The distribution of the colours on the palette is as follows:: colour 0 (zero), the transparent colour, is in the lower, right corner and colour 1 (one), the darkest colour in the shading range, is in the field on the left to it. The palette is read from right to left and from down upwards - but only with Adobe Photoshop, other graphics tools my differ completely on that issue (like DPaint), because each has its own palette representation. Thus, the other way around: the last colour, number 255, is on the upper left, the rest of the palette follows out usual reading direction counting downwards:
    254, 253, 252... okay, I can see, you're following me.

Step 3

    Chose IMAGE/MODE/COLOR TABLE.... The palette will appear and we may now begin to define our colours: simply click on one of the colour fields (best to begin with colour 0 on the lower right) and the menue will appear on the right (onyl half of it to begin with):

    To define the transparent and the darkest colour simply click on the "colours" white and black in the basic colours of the picker and chose OK. To define a new colour, click on one of the 16 free, white fields below the basic colours (preferably on the field on the upper left side which is already coloured red) and select Define colour. The fields Colour, Saturation and Light you can now use to define the desired colours.
    Start with colour 16, i.e. the field on the utter right in the last but one row, directly above colour 0 (You still follow me, don't you?). The rest of the first row you should keep available for colours that are not to be shaded - colour the fields pink to begin with (cf. basic colours, right row, middle) so that you may spot faulty pixels right away.

Step 4

    For the beginning (and the predesigned palette to be found further down) the following setting is recommended for colour defintion: a value of 240 for saturation for "real" colours, lower with washed ot colours and the value of 0 with shades of grey. The brightness of each colour should be available in 16 shades, starting with 230 and decreasing in steps of 14 down to 20, starting with the brightest colour on the right. The colour ranges should contain the shades 0, 16, 24, 32, 48, 64, 88, 104, 112, 132, 152, 176, 184, 224 and a range of shades of grey. As defining 256 colours does require quite a bit of keyboard work you should now and then choose OK and save the file in the PCX format. You may then include it in your WDL file with PALETTE Name {PALFILE <Name.PCX>;}. The palette is now only missing the following ranges:

RANGE 48,16; RANGE 64,16; RANGE 80,16; RANGE 96,16; RANGE 112,16; RANGE 128,16; RANGE 144,16; RANGE 160,16; RANGE 176,16; RANGE 192,16; RANGE 208,16; RANGE 224,16; RANGE 240,16;

And now...

    ...your palette should look like this one here. All clear? Okay, forget about DIY and simply download our prefabricated palette PALETTE. (The colours 2 to 6 are already defined for use in the Automap, green for the Player, yellow for the actors, red for Things and shades of grey for the walls). That doesn't mean there is an end to defining, because not every image you introduce in your game is supposed to contain all colours included in the palette. Suppose you scan in an egyptian wall painting with lots of red and brown and assign it the palette PALETTE.PCX with your graphics tool. Now it may occur that (because of the natural course of the colours among each other) colours will appear in the finished texture that don't want to be there (e.g. the colours 0 to 15 you should by all means delete from the palette!). So you will need additional special palettes apart from the general palette, colouring palettes.

    For that, mark all colours in the Color Table, that are not to be contained in the respective colouring palette and assign them the pink fault pixel (unless you are editing a piggy texture, in which case you might want to choose a different colour.... Save the palette as a separate ROTPAL.ACT, GRUENPAL.ACT etc. and load your image. Then choose IMAGE/MODE/RGB COLOR (if the image is alrady present in RGB) and immediately IMAGE/MODE/INDEXED COLOR - this change is elemental in getting the Adobe Photoshop to ask for a palette to return to 256 colours. Which is exactly where you step in and select CUSTOM and load the respective ACT file. Your image will then automatically be coloured with the colours of the palette.

      Good Luck!
