GAMES

PIKTO by Phaestos

It may sound greek, but Phaestos is a French company - one normally earning their daily baguettes by producing for TV. So how did they get it into their heads to produce a 3D Game? And who the hell is Pikto? Questions, questions - which will be answered in the Interview . This is only about the game itself. 

 

Intermediate Picture

 

Compared to that Pikto is not only well, but immaculately done!

PIKTO and ACKNEX:

 

Using ACKNEX for this game was, to avoid beating about the bush, a good choice. The engines features were well employed. One's movements are fluent and one does get the overall impression that the team has succeeded in understanding the TUTORIAL. Well done indeed! 

So why are there clipping errors? Was beta testing in need of hurry? And missing PORTCULLIES on lifts and the frequent appearance of objects stuck in the floor are completely unnecessary shortcomings. 

It is equally a pity that all Actors are twodimensional, Models would have been more impressive. But sadly that was not yet a feature of the version used here. 

And why for all the world are all beasts PASSABLE? I assume that they kept squeezing in the player and that it proved to be too annoying to accurately adjust Ways and Objects. So what the heck, they set them to PASSABLE. 

Having a shot Ammunitions or Health Icon - gotcha! - disappear may still be looked at as a feature. But I can't see the point in remorselessly causing all icons to disappear at IF_NEAR. Whoever gets to close to the respective symbol with a charged life- or ammunitions-depot kicks it out of the level and is left completely out at sea later when he needs them. The engine is thoroughly capable of reading the values of Health and Ammunition and - depending - leave those thingies wherever they are, so why? 

It is also not entirely clear why one can swim in some waters but is kept away by an invisible hand in front of others. 

The topography does not exactly kick ass, but is quite alright. 

The softly swinging  movments in the underwater levels are proof of a well designed Move-Rule. It sadly is a little annoying that one can't turn around quickly enough, but then we are under water.  

So let's simply have a comparing look at the smashin success of "Tumb Raider": the lousy pixel grafics make your eyes water. The whole thing swarms with clipping and regions errors. Especially below-regions apparently were an unsolvable, but mischieveously ignored problem. The topographs are far from being an example and last but not least: adversaries that have kicked the bucket are floating in the air and are PASSABLE. Still, the thing sells like mad. 


 

Artists' Atelier

 

But considering the highlights appearing once in a while even concerning the field of graphics - especially with a few objects and background textures - one does see that the creators could have done better, had they wished to do so.

Graphics:

We have already let it be known that we would have preferred models, as well as that it is not PHAESTOS' fault.   

But that's why the actors could have turned out to be a bit better. You can't always make excuses with reference to the comic-strip character of the game. The actors aren't exactly downright awful, but - and a few in this game prove the point - one can do better!

Really unpleasant are a few of the wall's graphics. They may send shivers of distaste down your spine. The neolithic rocks were probably designed on an equally old computer by a neophytic assistant commercial artist.

 

Circus Ring

 

Music:

Maybe we were simply lucky with the choice of our test level, because apart from some annoying circus music we only were longing to press F12 in the artists world. Generally the music is simply good, athmospherically well suited to the level. It gets even better when the music is subdued in favour of bird's calls and similar sounds. That's probably where their TV experience shows.

Sound:

Sorry for malcontents: can't find no fault here. There may be a slight overload of "tshing" and "uihoops" and "boing", but the sounds are perfectly suited to the setting are a well done.

Equally accessible only with difficulty is the operation during the game. Initially I started shooting to no avail, because I am used to hitting the Ctrl. button when I want to shoot down an opponent. 

User Interface and Menu:

There are one main- and several sub-menues. They are all filling the entire screen. But to people like me the graphically well done, two-dimensional comic strips intially mean nothing. With my knowledge of French approaching the non-existent neither could I turn to the handbook. So it was trial and clicking. One's bound to find out sooner or later. But it's not that simple. After some annoying ado I succeeded in setting a play's name for myself. Later in the game one realises that this is also the only way to "save" anything. Quicksave one seeks in vain. Even if you bother with saving anything labouriously using the menu, you won't be lucky. First you choose name and level of severity and then saving is carried out remorselessly. One game and level of severity to each name. If you die you are sent back to the start of the respective level. Saving first and opening the door afterwards can't be done. 

After solving this riddle I also found out what a "Balade" mode is: if you click on the figure running across the upper screen you are given a choice of four levels. You can now chose one and are transported into it (in my case once even onto a roof). You can take a walk through the level and - really nice idea - fly a baloon over it. But you will look in vain for opponents, and you are doomed to remain inactive. "Balade" made me rather "Malade" until I got the hang of it: you can look, but you can't touch! Some kind of appetizer, once you know that. 

If you want to play, you have to click on the kicking fellow. Here as well you can choose one of the creatures (statues) and thus enter the respective level. once you are there things are done one after the other. So if you enjoyed the third level in the stone age you have to get through one and two first. Not at all bad, 's probably what one calls motivation. 

The three levels of severity only distinguish themselves by the number of opponents. As you are allowed to choose the different world in any order you are only chained to a certain level of severity within the four levels proprietary to the world.

But that, sadly, causes "rien" to happen. It cost me a few lives before I got used to the Space Bar as a shooting button and finally was enabled to defend myself.  

And to use ENTER of all things to open doors and pick up or put down objects is rather original. The French probably translated the word verbatim. You can choose your weapons with 1,2, or 3 - quite the way you are used to. But I did not find a way to toggle.

 

Attack of the Locusts

 

The Game:

After getting used to the menu, we get down to the job and conquer the various worlds of Pikto. We're beamed into a colourful building, intially facing a huge video-wall, probably depicting Pikto. Then, suddenly, we are turned around by 180 degrees and find ourselves face to face with the statue of a green locust. If we now approach, pressing the space bar (instead of Enter, as might be expected) we find ourselves in the first level of the world of locusts, bugs and generally creepy-crawly things. 

But we don't have to - we are allowed the freedom to wander around a bit. Which is when we discover, probably because we tumble down the very same, a stairway. It is graphically and topographically not exactly a masterpiece. So what. At least there are several rooms filled with larger or smaller numbers of statues representing the existing worlds. Each one contains four successive levels. Motionless they sit on their pedestals, staring down, waiting to be chosen. 

The problems waiting for us are not exactly varied, but that's not really the point. There is one simple puzzle to be solved in each level: How do I attain the symbol blocked by some creature of the level?
So basically it's about shootin'. But that is done in a very original way. Each world has its own choice of three weapons. To battle the bugs you are for example supplied with a pump or sprayer filled with insecticide. The poisonous mushrooms are even more effective. But they sadly are rarer, still you can re-use them if missing the target, and they are guaranteed to knock out even the
strongest bug. Attacking painters or aggressive musicians can be countered with brushes hurling deadly colour blots. Other proven devices include shrill sounds or CDs. But before I lose myself going on about neolithical chestnut-throwers and boomerangs, cards in the world of clowns and garlic against denture-throwing vampires: The ideas are a smash! And they work reliably!s

 

 

Port town

 

The game does sport really good gags and is fun! Many marginal details (flying birds, whales swimming past etc.) additionally enliven the levels. Not only children enjoy shooting with cameras at fish-throwing seamen. Even grown-ups may find ants lifting weigth in a bug's fitness studio or a sealion's cinema with a fin-clapping audience amusing.

The Fun Factor:

Once you get used to the unique control and the lack of ways to save, Pikto can be a lot of fun! There is a point when you stop thinking about the deeper sense of it all. After all it is totally irrelevant who Pikto actually is. The puzzles are hardly worthy of that name, but then again: children are the main target group.
